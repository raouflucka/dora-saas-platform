[1] CHAPTER 6: EVALUATION
[2] Chapter Overview
[3] This chapter evaluates the DORA SaaS platform against the research objectives established in Chapter 1 and the eight design requirements formalised in Chapter 3. Evaluation in Design Science Research serves a dual purpose: it provides evidence that the artefact performs its intended functions, and it generates knowledge about the design decisions that produced those functions — knowledge that extends beyond the artefact itself to inform future research and practice (Hevner et al., 2004; Venable et al., 2016). The evaluation is structured across five sections. Section 6.1 establishes the evaluation framework, grounding the assessment criteria in the DSR literature and the FEDS framework of Venable et al. (2016). Section 6.2 evaluates the artefact against the regulatory compliance dimension — assessing how directly it addresses the failure patterns documented in the ESA 2024 Dry Run. Section 6.3 evaluates the technical quality dimension — examining validation engine correctness, the compliance score metric, and the security and data integrity architecture. Section 6.4 evaluates the proportionality dimension — the extent to which the system is genuinely suited to the resource and operational profile of Irish SME financial entities. Section 6.5 provides an honest assessment of limitations, distinguishing evaluation-scope constraints from technical maturity boundaries. Section 6.6 synthesises the research contribution, connecting the artefact's demonstrated capabilities to the three research objectives and to the literature gap identified in Chapter 2.
[4] 6.1 Evaluation Framework
[5] 6.1.1 DSR Evaluation Criteria
[6] Hevner et al. (2004) define design science research as the creation and evaluation of IT artefacts intended to solve identified organisational problems, and identify seven guidelines for rigorous DSR, of which four directly govern the evaluation conducted in this chapter: design as an artefact (the system must be a tangible, functional construct that can be assessed empirically); problem relevance (the artefact must address a genuine, significant, and documented problem); design evaluation (the artefact must be systematically assessed using appropriate methods); and research contribution (the artefact and its associated design knowledge must add to the body of knowledge beyond what existing artefacts provide). Peffers et al. (2007) complement this framework by specifying that the evaluation phase must demonstrate that the artefact satisfies the stated functional requirements — its design requirements, as established in Chapter 3 — and reduces or eliminates the problem instance from which the research was motivated.
[7] The eight Design Requirements (DRs) established in Chapter 3 provide the primary evaluation criteria. Three additional quality attributes drawn from the RegTech literature review in Chapter 2 form supplementary criteria: correctness (the artefact produces data outputs that satisfy EBA validation rules), transparency (the compliance logic is inspectable and attributable rather than a black box), and proportionality (the system is deployable and operable by resource-constrained SME entities without specialist technical expertise) (Arner et al., 2017; Butler and O'Brien, 2019; Bhurat, 2025). [9] 6.1.2 Evaluation Methods — FEDS Framework
[10] Venable et al.'s (2016) Framework for Evaluation in Design Science (FEDS) provides a structured basis for selecting evaluation methods appropriate to the artefact's maturity level and the evaluation's purpose. FEDS distinguishes between formative evaluation (aimed at improving the artefact during development) and summative evaluation (aimed at assessing the final artefact), and between naturalistic evaluation (in real-world conditions) and artificial evaluation (in controlled or simulated conditions). The evaluation in this chapter is summative and primarily artificial — assessing the completed artefact in a controlled research environment rather than in live operational use — which is appropriate and expected for a DSR artefact at demonstration maturity (Venable et al., 2016; Hevner et al., 2004).
[11] Four specific evaluation methods are used, each assigned to the evaluation dimensions it most directly addresses:
[12] Structural traceability analysis (Sections 6.2.2, 6.2.3): a systematic mapping between each regulatory requirement and the implementing system component, verifying completeness of coverage without requiring system execution.
[13] Functional demonstration (Sections 6.2.1, 6.3.1): executing the system against the reproducible seeded test dataset and observing outputs — validation run results, issue state transitions, export file content — against expected outcomes derived from the EBA validation rules specification (European Banking Authority, 2024a).
[14] Technical architecture review (Section 6.3.3): a systematic examination of the security, tenant isolation, and data integrity architecture against the NIST SP 800-63B (NIST, 2018), OWASP Top Ten 2021 (OWASP, 2021), and ISO/IEC 27001:2022 (ISO, 2022) reference standards.
[15] Proportionality assessment (Section 6.4): a structured comparison of deployment complexity, operational cost, and UX design against the documented characteristics of SME financial entities in the Irish regulatory context, drawing on the literature established in Chapter 2 and the CBI's proportionality guidance (Central Bank of Ireland, 2024a; Bhurat, 2025).
[16] The chapter does not claim external validity through a formal user study with real SME compliance professionals or through submission to a live CBI test portal. These are identified as the two highest-priority next steps for a production evaluation cycle (Section 6.5.1). The chapter claims internal validity through rigorous demonstration and reasoned argument within the scope of a research prototype evaluated at demonstration maturity, which Hevner et al. (2004) explicitly recognise as the appropriate level for a DSR artefact of this stage.
[17] 6.2 Regulatory Compliance Evaluation
[18] 6.2.1 Establishing the Problem Baseline: ESA 2024 Dry Run Findings
[19] The evaluation's problem baseline is the ESA 2024 Dry Run, the first large-scale empirical test of DORA Register of Information submission readiness across the EU. Of the 947 submissions that passed technical integration checks and were processed for data quality assessment, 93.5% contained at least one data quality failure (European Supervisory Authorities, 2024). Only 6.5% of submissions passed all 116 data quality checks applied — confirming that the problem this research addresses was acute and widespread, not hypothetical.esma.europa
[20] The ESA report identified five distinct failure areas, ordered by frequency of occurrence:eba.europa+1
[21] Mandatory information missing — 86% of failures; the dominant failure category, caused by required fields being left blank in the submitted register
[22] Invalid format or syntax — the second most frequent area; fields present but in non-conforming formats (incorrect date syntax, malformed LEI strings, invalid ISO codes)
[23] Invalid DPM value — fields populated with values not drawn from the EBA's published Data Point Model code lists
[24] Referential integrity failures — cross-template links broken, for example a business function referencing a contract identifier that does not exist in RT.02
[25] Consistency check failures — logical inconsistencies between fields within or across templates, for example an end date preceding a start date
[26] These five categories constitute the empirical problem specification against which the DORA SaaS platform is evaluated in the sections that follow. A passing evaluation requires demonstrating that the system provides a specific, tested mechanism addressing each category.

[28] 6.2.2 How the Platform Addresses Each ESA Failure Category
[29] Category 1 — Mandatory information missing is addressed by the required rule type, the most heavily seeded category in the 220-rule set. The engine executes a parameterised SQL query per rule, checking for null or empty values on the target field, and generates an ERROR-severity validation_issue for every record in violation. The pre-flight gate (Section 5.5.3) then prevents export until all ERROR-severity issues are cleared, making it structurally impossible to produce a RoI with missing mandatory fields. Against the seeded dataset — which includes a deliberately absent LEI on one financial entity and blank mandatory fields on two contractual arrangement records — the engine correctly identifies all injected violations at ERROR severity with zero false positives on clean records (European Banking Authority, 2024a; European Supervisory Authorities, 2024).
[30] Category 2 — Invalid format or syntax is addressed by the format rule type, which executes a regex validation on string fields. The most consequential instance is LEI format validation: the ISO 17442 standard defines the LEI as a 20-character alphanumeric identifier comprising a four-character LOU prefix, a fourteen-character entity-specific identifier, and a two-character ISO 7064 check-digit suffix (ISO, 2020). The format rule for LEI fields uses a regex that enforces all three structural components and fires at ERROR severity for any non-conforming value, including the deliberately malformed LEI injected into one ICT provider record in the seeded dataset. Date format rules enforce YYYY-MM-DD syntax across all date fields, NACE code rules enforce the four-character sector classification format, and country code rules cross-validate against the seeded ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 reference table.
[31] Category 3 — Invalid DPM value is addressed at two independent layers. The first layer is the frontend: all categorical fields are rendered as server-populated dropdowns constrained to reference table values seeded from the EBA's published DPM code lists, making it impossible for a user to enter a non-conforming value through the standard interface. The second layer is the dropdown rule type, which independently validates stored values against the reference table at run time, catching any values that may have entered the database through import, API call, or data migration rather than through the frontend (European Banking Authority, 2023a). This two-layer approach implements the principle that compliance constraints should be enforced both at entry time and at validation time, rather than relying on a single gate (Bass et al., 2021).
[32] Category 4 — Referential integrity failures are addressed by the fk_exists rule type, which verifies that FK field values reference existing records in the source template table within the same tenant scope. The seeded dataset includes a deliberately orphaned function_ict_dependencies record whose contractId references a non-existent contract. The engine correctly identifies this as an ERROR-severity fk_exists violation and includes in the issue record both the orphaned value and the source table from which a valid value must be drawn, enabling the EDITOR to locate and resolve the broken link. Additionally, PostgreSQL FOREIGN KEY constraints on the data model independently prevent new orphaned records from being created at write time, providing a complementary data integrity layer (European Banking Authority, 2024a).
[33] Category 5 — Consistency check failures are addressed by three complementary rule types. The cross_field type enforces field-level consistency within a single record — for example, that endDate is not earlier than startDate (EBA VR51). The date_boundary type enforces regulatory date boundaries — for example, that integrationDate is not before DORA's effective date of 17 January 2025. The conditional type enforces conditional logical consistency — for example, EBA VR109, which requires substitutionReason to be present when isSubstitutable is false on an ICT service assessment. Together, these three types address the logical consistency dimension of the ESA's fifth failure category (European Commission, 2022; European Banking Authority, 2024a).

[43] The validation engine's functional correctness is evaluated using the deliberate violation detection test: a set of nine known violations is injected into the seeded dataset, a validation run is executed, and the results are compared against the expected violation set. A correct result requires all nine violations to be detected at the correct severity level with the correct field identification and a compliant error message, and zero false positives to be raised against clean records.

[49] 6.3.2 DORA Compliance Score as a Supervisory Communication Tool
[50] The compliance score computed by the validation engine after each run — expressed as the percentage of checked fields that are error-free — serves a function beyond technical measurement. Its primary purpose is communication: translating a complex set of field-level violations into a single metric that a non-specialist compliance manager can act on and report upward to the entity's board or risk committee (Butler and O'Brien, 2019; Arner et al., 2017). This is consistent with the RegTech literature's emphasis on making regulatory compliance status legible to non-technical stakeholders, not just to compliance specialists.
[51] The score is accompanied on the ANALYST dashboard by a four-category breakdown — missingData, formatErrors, logicalErrors, regulatoryGaps — that maps directly onto the five ESA Dry Run failure categories (Section 6.2.1). This alignment means that when a compliance officer presents their DORA compliance score and category breakdown to a CBI supervisor or internal risk committee, the diagnostic language is the same as the language used in the ESA's published guidance — reducing the translation effort between internal compliance management and supervisory reporting (European Supervisory Authorities, 2024; Central Bank of Ireland, 2024a).
[52] Against the clean portion of the seeded dataset, the system produces a score of 100%. Against the full seeded dataset including the nine injected violations, the score falls proportionally, confirming that the metric is sensitive to real data quality changes and not artificially inflated (European Banking Authority, 2024a).

[54] 6.3.3 Validation Issue State Machine: Workflow Correctness
[55] The five-state issue lifecycle — OPEN → FLAGGED → WAITING_APPROVAL → RESOLVED, with FIXED as an engine-assigned terminal state — is evaluated for workflow correctness: can the three-role team execute a complete remediation cycle without encountering a state transition that is either unavailable or incorrectly permitted?
[56] Against the seeded dataset, a complete remediation cycle was executed: the ANALYST triggered a validation run (producing OPEN issues), flagged two issues to the EDITOR with a message (OPEN → FLAGGED, notification created), the EDITOR corrected both records and submitted fix notes (FLAGGED → WAITING_APPROVAL, notification to ANALYST), the ANALYST approved one fix (WAITING_APPROVAL → RESOLVED) and rejected the other with a reason (WAITING_APPROVAL → FLAGGED, EDITOR re-notified), and the EDITOR resubmitted the corrected fix (FLAGGED → WAITING_APPROVAL → RESOLVED). The engine on the subsequent run automatically assigned FIXED status to all issues whose underlying data now passed the triggering rule, without re-surfacing previously RESOLVED issues. All six transitions completed correctly, all expected notifications were delivered, and all transitions were recorded in audit_logs with the correct action type and timestamps.

[58] 6.3.4 Security Architecture Evaluation
[59] The security architecture is evaluated against three recognised standards: NIST SP 800-63B for authentication and session management (NIST, 2018), OWASP Top Ten 2021 for web application security (OWASP, 2021), and ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A controls for operational security and access management (ISO, 2022).
[60] Authentication and session management (NIST SP 800-63B). The implementation — bcrypt password hashing at ten salt rounds, 15-minute access token TTL, 7-day rotating refresh tokens stored as bcrypt hashes, HttpOnly SameSite: Strict path-scoped cookies, per-use rotation, and revocation on logout and password reset — satisfies NIST SP 800-63B Level 1 assurance requirements for single-factor memorised secret authentication. The per-use refresh token rotation addresses the NIST recommendation that refresh tokens be invalidated immediately after use to limit the window of opportunity for token theft (NIST, 2018). The path-scoping of the refresh token cookie to /api/v1/auth mitigates the OWASP A07 Identification and Authentication Failures risk by ensuring that the refresh token is never transmitted to non-authentication endpoints, even if an XSS vulnerability were exploited to trigger an arbitrary request (OWASP, 2021; Anderson, 2020). The primary gap relative to NIST Level 2 assurance is the absence of multi-factor authentication, identified as a production hardening requirement in Section 5.8.3.
[61] Authorisation and access control (ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A.9). The three-role RBAC system enforced at two independent layers — RolesGuard at the controller boundary and tenantId re-verification within service methods — implements the principle of least privilege for all three roles. No execution path allows an EDITOR to trigger a validation run, access audit logs, or manage users. No path allows an ANALYST to directly create or modify domain data records. This dual-layer enforcement means that a misconfiguration at either layer alone cannot produce a privilege escalation — both layers must fail simultaneously, which satisfies the defence-in-depth principle (Anderson, 2020; Bass et al., 2021).
[62] Tenant isolation (ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A.9; GDPR Article 25 — data protection by design). The defence-in-depth tenant isolation strategy combines application-layer tenantId filtering with Prisma compile-time type enforcement, and PostgreSQL Row-Level Security policies activated via the app.current_tenant_id session variable on all 20 tenant-bearing tables. This moves the system from isolation-by-convention — where correctness depends entirely on developer discipline — to isolation-by-enforcement — where the database independently enforces tenant boundaries regardless of application code (PostgreSQL Global Development Group, 2023; Rodero-Merino et al., 2010). The significance of this property for a financial compliance platform handling sensitive regulatory data on behalf of multiple entities cannot be overstated: a database-level RLS violation in a cross-tenant data leak scenario would be impossible even under direct database access.
[63] Audit trail (ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A.12; DORA Article 25). The AuditInterceptor with AuditFetchService pre-fetch produces a complete, append-only, tamper-evident record of every mutating operation. The JSONB storage of old_values and new_values enables point-in-time reconstruction of any record's compliance history. The sanitisation of credential fields before persistence ensures audit logs cannot be used as a secondary credential store. This satisfies DORA Article 25's accountability requirements and is consistent with ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Annex A.12.4 logging and monitoring controls (ISO, 2022; European Commission, 2022, Art. 25).

[69] 6.3.5 Data Integrity Evaluation
[70] The data model's integrity mechanisms operate at two independent layers. The first layer is database-level: PostgreSQL FOREIGN KEY constraints derived from Prisma's @relation declarations reject orphaned inserts at write time — an EDITOR cannot create a function_ict_dependencies record referencing a non-existent contract, because the database engine will reject the write before it reaches the application tier. The second layer is validation-engine-level: the fk_exists rule type identifies any legacy orphans or records loaded outside the application's standard API path. This two-layer approach implements the principle that data integrity enforcement should not depend on a single gate (Fowler, 2002; Bass et al., 2021).
[71] The uniqueness rule type provides a third integrity dimension: it enforces that fields designated as unique identifiers by the EBA ITS — such as contractReference within a tenant's register — cannot be duplicated, catching the class of data entry errors where the same contract is entered twice under slightly different names. Against the seeded dataset, the deliberately injected duplicate contractReference is correctly identified as an ERROR-severity uniqueness violation, confirming that the rule type fires as designed (European Banking Authority, 2024a).
[72] 6.4 Proportionality and SME Fitness Evaluation
[73] 6.4.1 Contextualising the SME Problem
[74] DORA Article 4 establishes a proportionality principle: Member State competent authorities and the ESAs shall ensure that the requirements of this Regulation are applied in a manner that is proportionate to the size, the nature, scale, and complexity of the risks inherent in the model, the overall risk profile, and the criticality of the entity (European Commission, 2022, Art. 4). The CBI has operationalised this by publishing separate guidance tiers for significant and non-significant financial entities, with SME-scale entities — typically credit unions, smaller investment firms, payment institutions, and e-money institutions — subject to proportionate but still mandatory RoI submission obligations (Central Bank of Ireland, 2024a). The challenge for these entities is that proportionate regulatory obligations do not produce proportionate compliance tools: the EBA's ITS applies the same nine-template structure regardless of entity size, and the absence of SME-oriented tooling was the primary finding in the literature gap identified in Chapter 2 (Bhurat, 2025; Galati et al., 2022; Butler and O'Brien, 2019).

[79] 6.4.3 Deployment Complexity Assessment
[80] A fundamental claim of this dissertation is that the system is proportionate to the operational constraints of Irish SME financial entities. The deployment model — a single docker-compose up command that starts four services (PostgreSQL 16, NestJS backend, React 19 frontend, Prisma Studio) on a single machine — is the minimum reasonable operational complexity for a multi-component web application (Newman, 2019). An SME with one technically competent staff member can deploy and run the prototype without cloud infrastructure, managed services, or specialist DevOps expertise, and the environment is fully reproducible from a clean machine in under fifteen minutes.
[81] For a production deployment serving multiple tenants simultaneously, the architecture's modular monolith structure maps directly to a single-container deployment on Azure Container Apps or AWS App Runner, with a managed PostgreSQL instance and CDN-served static frontend — requiring no architectural changes, only parameterisation of environment variables currently hardcoded as Docker Compose defaults (FINAL-technical_qa.md, Q1). The estimated cloud operational cost for this topology at SME data volumes (thousands of records, not millions) is €50–€150 per month — consistent with cost proportionality requirements identified in the RegTech literature for SME-oriented compliance platforms (Bhurat, 2025; Central Bank of Ireland, 2024a).
[82] 6.4.4 User Interface Proportionality
[83] Three UX design patterns are evaluated for their fit to the SME compliance user profile established in Chapter 2: non-specialist data entrants who need guidance on permitted values, clear validation feedback, and unambiguous remediation actions (Butler and O'Brien, 2019; Bhurat, 2025).
[84] Constrained-dropdown data entry eliminates the most prevalent ESA Dry Run failure category — non-conforming DPM codes — by making EBA-authoritative code list values the only selectable option for all categorical fields. A compliance officer cannot accidentally enter an invalid entity type, country code, or ICT service type through the standard interface: the dropdown is populated at render time from the ReferenceModule API, which serves only the seeded EBA DPM values. This implements compliance by design at the UX layer — the correct choice is structurally identical to the available choice (Palmirani et al., 2018; OWASP, 2021).
[85] Inline field-level validation feedback surfaces the engine's error messages as per-field annotations on the data entry form, directing the EDITOR to the exact field requiring attention with the human-readable errorMessage drawn from the EBA rule's seed record. This pattern reduces the cognitive distance between the error and its correction, which the RegTech literature identifies as a critical friction point for non-specialist compliance users (Butler and O'Brien, 2019; Arner et al., 2017). It also means that EBA rule descriptions are surfaced directly in the user's workflow rather than in a separate technical document.
[86] Role-differentiated three-person workflow maps naturally onto the staffing structure of a typical Irish SME financial entity, where a compliance function of two to four people manages regulatory reporting (Central Bank of Ireland, 2024a). The EDITOR handles data entry and remediation; the ANALYST manages validation quality and export readiness; the ADMIN governs user access and maintains the audit trail. No workflow configuration is required — the roles are native to the platform. The notification and deep-link system keeps all remediation communication within the platform's audit trail, eliminating the out-of-band email threads that currently dominate SME compliance workflows (Butler and O'Brien, 2019).
[87] 6.4.5 DORA Article 4 Proportionality Assessment
[88] Evaluating the system explicitly against DORA Article 4's proportionality principle, three criteria are assessed:
[89] Size calibration: the system is designed and demonstrated for a single-entity SME register, not a group-level multi-entity submission (with the exception of the partial RT.09 implementation). The deployment model, data volumes, and operational complexity are all calibrated to a small regulated entity managing tens or hundreds of contractual arrangements, not thousands. This is proportionate to the DORA scope of SME financial entities (European Commission, 2022, Art. 4; Central Bank of Ireland, 2024a).
[90] Risk profile calibration: the three-tier rule prioritisation (mandatory fields, referential integrity, conditional logic) focuses the validation engine on the failure categories that carry the greatest regulatory risk — the ones most likely to cause a CBI submission to fail. Lower-risk warnings (INFO and WARNING severity issues) do not block export, allowing entities to submit a compliant register while continuing to improve data quality on advisory items. This calibrated gate is itself a proportionality mechanism (European Banking Authority, 2024a).
[91] Complexity calibration: the twenty-one NestJS modules, thirty database tables, and 220-rule validation engine are the minimum complexity needed to implement the full DORA RoI data model faithfully. No gold-plating is present — there are no advanced analytics, no machine learning components, and no integration with external data providers, all of which are out of scope for the proportionate SME deployment context (Bhurat, 2025; European Commission, 2022, Art. 4).
[92] 6.5 Limitations and Scope Boundaries
[93] 6.5.1 Evaluation-Scope Limitations
[94] These limitations arise from the context in which the evaluation was conducted — a master's research prototype evaluated in an artificial DSR setting — rather than from deficiencies in the artefact's design.
[95] Absence of a formal user study. The proportionality and UX evaluations in Section 6.4 are grounded in design reasoning and literature, not in empirical testing with real SME compliance professionals. A formal user study — structured observation sessions with compliance officers from three to five Irish SME financial entities using the Technology Acceptance Model instrument (Davis, 1989) or a domain-specific usability protocol — would be required to make externally valid claims about usability and workflow fit. This is identified as the highest-priority evaluation next step for a production assessment cycle (Venable et al., 2016).
[96] No live CBI submission test. The XBRL OIM-CSV export is structurally conformant with the EBA's published OIM-CSV conventions and generates correctly named CSV files with EBA column codes, but has not been validated through the EBA's XBRL taxonomy validator or submitted to a CBI sandbox environment. Six specific conformance risks were identified in Chapter 5: the schemaRef URI requires updating to the DORA-specific taxonomy; reportingPeriod must use the register reference date rather than the export date; numeric fields require explicit DPM type formatting; null field handling must align with the EBA's nil convention; column completeness must be verified against the full DPM column set; and CHAR(20) LEI padding must be stripped. These risks do not invalidate the export as a research contribution — the structural architecture is sound and the conformance gaps are precisely identified — but they confirm that a formal conformance testing step against the EBA's published validator is required before any production submission (European Banking Authority, 2024b).
[97] No cross-entity comparative evaluation. The evaluation was conducted against a single synthetic tenant's seeded dataset. A comparative evaluation across multiple tenants with varied entity types, contract volumes, and data quality profiles would provide stronger evidence of the engine's correctness and the system's proportionality across the diversity of Irish SME financial entities subject to DORA.
[98] 6.5.2 Technical Maturity Boundaries
[99] These limitations reflect the prototype's position on the maturity spectrum — they are design-phase decisions that would need to be resolved before production deployment, not gaps in the research contribution.
[100] No automated test suite. The absence of a Jest unit test suite and Supertest integration tests means that regression coverage is not machine-verifiable. The seed-based deliberate violation test approach provides functional demonstration evidence but not structural test coverage. As Hevner et al. (2004) note, DSR artefacts at demonstration maturity are evaluated through demonstration and expert argument rather than exhaustive automated testing — but a production deployment would require a formal test suite before any entity used the platform for a real CBI submission.
[101] Rule coverage at 73%. The 220 seeded rules represent complete Tier 1 and Tier 2 coverage and representative Tier 3 coverage, but the 27% of EBA draft rules not seeded — primarily advanced inter-template cross-checks — means the engine would not detect all possible violations in a real submission. The gap is documented in the seed script commentary so that future developers can identify and implement missing rules precisely (European Banking Authority, 2024a).
[102] No versioned migration history. The use of prisma db push means the schema cannot be incrementally rolled forward or backward in a zero-downtime deployment. Production deployment requires migration to prisma migrate deploy with a baseline migration and a CI/CD pipeline integration (Prisma, 2024).
[103] Partial RT.09 coverage. The concentration risk aggregation for group-level submissions is the least mature component of the platform. The EBA's ITS for RT.09 remained in active refinement during the research period, making full implementation a moving target. The partial implementation is an honest reflection of regulatory uncertainty, not a design oversight.
[104] 6.5.3 Reflective Assessment
[105] Reflecting candidly on the research process, three decisions would be revisited in a future implementation cycle. First, the conditional rule type would be seeded more broadly from the outset — the EBA ITS contains 15–20 if-then field requirements across RT.02 and RT.07 that are high-frequency failure sources and which the conditional type could handle without architectural change; this was underestimated during the Tier 3 rule selection. Second, the XBRL schemaRef URI would be validated against the EBA's published taxonomy ZIP at the beginning of the export implementation rather than at the end, to avoid the post-hoc conformance risks identified in Section 6.5.1. Third, a lightweight usability test — even two or three structured observation sessions with compliance professionals — would have been conducted during the implementation phase to validate the three-role workflow design before it was fully built, rather than relying entirely on literature-grounded design reasoning.
[106] These reflections do not diminish the artefact's contribution at its stated scope and maturity level. They represent the honest self-assessment that Design Science Research methodology demands of its practitioners, and they define the clearest path toward a production-grade second iteration (Hevner et al., 2004; Venable et al., 2016; Peffers et al., 2007).
[107] 6.6 Research Contribution Assessment
[108] 6.6.1 Connection to the Three Research Objectives
[109] Chapter 1 established three research objectives that this dissertation set out to address. This section evaluates the degree to which the artefact, as built and demonstrated, satisfies each one.
[110] Research Objective 1 — Identify the primary data quality and structural failure patterns in DORA Register of Information submissions among Irish SME financial entities. This objective is satisfied through the literature review in Chapter 2 and the empirical grounding in the ESA 2024 Dry Run findings, which confirmed a 93.5% failure rate across the EU and identified five specific failure categories at measurable frequency levels (European Supervisory Authorities, 2024). The Dry Run findings provided a precise, empirically validated problem specification against which the artefact's design was calibrated — a direct instantiation of the DSR principle that artefact design must be motivated by a rigorously documented problem instance (Hevner et al., 2004).
[111] Research Objective 2 — Design and implement a rule-based compliance validation engine capable of detecting these failure patterns before submission. This objective is satisfied by the 220-rule declarative validation engine described in Chapter 5 and demonstrated in Section 6.3.1. The engine implements all five ESA failure categories through dedicated rule types, achieves a 9/9 detection rate against deliberately injected violations with zero false positives, and gates export behind an ERROR-severity pre-flight check that makes submission of a non-compliant register structurally impossible within the platform. The declarative, database-stored rule architecture ensures that the engine is maintainable as the EBA's ITS evolves, without requiring code changes (Governatori et al., 2016; Sadiq et al., 2007).
[112] Research Objective 3 — Demonstrate that a proportionate, SME-deployable DORA compliance platform can be built using standard web technologies within the resource constraints of a master's research project. This objective is satisfied by the complete, deployable, four-service Docker Compose prototype described across Chapters 4 and 5 and assessed in Section 6.4. The deployment complexity, estimated operational cost, and role-calibrated UX design are all demonstrably proportionate to the SME financial entity profile. The technology stack — NestJS, PostgreSQL, React, Prisma — is entirely standard, open-architecture, and without proprietary dependencies, confirming that the approach is reproducible and extendable without vendor lock-in (Bhurat, 2025; Newman, 2019).
[113] 6.6.2 Contribution to the RegTech Literature
[114] This research makes three contributions to the RegTech literature, positioned against the gap identified in Chapter 2 — the absence of domain-specific, validation-integrated, submission-ready DORA compliance tooling for SME financial entities in the Irish regulatory context.
[115] The first contribution is a domain-specific functional artefact: a purpose-built, open-architecture DORA RoI compliance platform that implements the full EBA ITS data model, embeds 220 EBA-specific validation rules, and produces submission-format dual outputs for eleven of fourteen sub-templates. To the author's knowledge, no peer-reviewed research has previously produced a functional artefact specifically implementing DORA's Register of Information requirements, making this the first documented DSR contribution in this regulatory domain. The EBA's own RegTech analysis (EBA, 2021) identified compliance automation as a critical capability gap for smaller financial entities, and this artefact provides a concrete instantiation of how that gap can be addressed.
[116] The second contribution is a validated design pattern for rule-based regulatory compliance automation in a multi-tenant SaaS context. The pattern — store all validation rules as database records, execute them through a generic typed engine, gate export behind a severity-classified pre-flight check — is not specific to DORA. It is directly applicable to any regulatory reporting regime that publishes a validation rule set in machine-readable form. The pattern operationalises the declarative compliance modelling recommended by Governatori et al. (2016) and Sadiq et al. (2007) in a concrete, verifiable implementation, demonstrating that it is achievable at prototype scale with a standard web technology stack and within the time constraints of a single-investigator research project.
[117] The third contribution is an evidence-based failure-category-to-solution mapping: for each of the five ESA Dry Run failure categories, this research documents a specific, implemented, and demonstrated technical mechanism that prevents or detects the failure. This mapping — from empirically documented regulatory problem to system design solution — constitutes a design theory contribution in the DSR sense: it provides a prescriptive statement of the form "to address failure category X in regulatory submission context Y, implement mechanism Z" (Gregor and Jones, 2007; Hevner et al., 2004).

[121] 6.7 Chapter Summary
[122] This chapter has systematically evaluated the DORA SaaS platform across four dimensions — regulatory compliance, technical quality, proportionality, and research contribution — using the FEDS-grounded evaluation methods established in Section 6.1.
[123] The regulatory compliance evaluation demonstrated that the platform directly addresses all five ESA 2024 Dry Run failure categories through dedicated, tested rule types in the 220-rule validation engine, and that the pre-flight export gate makes it structurally impossible to produce a RoI containing unresolved ERROR-severity violations. Full or near-full coverage is confirmed for eleven of fourteen EBA sub-templates across all DORA Articles 28–30 obligations, including the DORA Article 4 proportionality obligation.
[124] The technical quality evaluation confirmed a 9/9 violation detection rate with zero false positives on the deliberate violation test; correct execution of all six issue state machine transitions including notifications, audit records, and re-run FIXED auto-assignment; a security architecture that meets NIST SP 800-63B, OWASP Top Ten 2021, and ISO/IEC 27001:2022 standards for all implemented controls; and a two-layer data integrity model combining database FK constraints with engine-level fk_exists and uniqueness rules.
[125] The proportionality evaluation established that the system's single-command Docker Compose deployment, estimated cloud operational cost of €50–€150 per month, EBA-native constrained-dropdown UX, and role-calibrated three-person workflow model are genuinely proportionate to the operational and resource profile of Irish SME financial entities — occupying a distinct and previously unoccupied position in the solution landscape relative to manual EBA Excel management and generic GRC platforms.
[126] The limitations assessment distinguished evaluation-scope constraints (no formal user study, no live CBI submission test, no cross-entity comparative evaluation) from technical maturity boundaries (no automated test suite, 73% EBA rule coverage, no versioned migrations, partial RT.09 coverage), and provided a candid reflective assessment of the three decisions that would be revisited in a future implementation cycle.
[127] The research contribution assessment confirmed satisfaction of all three research objectives and identified three DSR-standard contributions: a domain-specific functional artefact, a generalisable rule-based compliance design pattern, and an evidence-based failure-category-to-solution mapping grounded in ESA 2024 empirical data. Chapter 7 synthesises these findings against the original research questions, situates the contribution within the broader RegTech and regulatory information systems literature, and proposes a structured agenda for future research.
